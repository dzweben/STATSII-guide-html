Problem 6 prompt (from SG3)

We are looking at subjects’ ability to recall characters in a string based on (a) the type of string (digits/letters/mixed — within-subject factor) and (b) the type of brain damage the subject suffered (left brain / right brain / control — between-subject factor). The experiment is using a 3×3 design; n = 18. Note: when we tested assumptions we found equality of variances as well as equality of the covariance/variance matrices.

Notation + standard equations (used throughout)

What the symbols mean

  • \(Y_{ijk}\): recall score for subject \(k\) in damage group \(i\) at stimuli level \(j\).
  • \(\bar Y_{\cdot\cdot\cdot}\): grand mean (given: 7.48).
  • \(\bar Y_{i\cdot\cdot}\): damage marginal mean for group \(i\) (averaged over stimuli).
  • \(\bar Y_{\cdot j \cdot}\): stimuli marginal mean for level \(j\) (averaged over damage).
  • \(\bar Y_{ij\cdot}\): cell mean for damage \(i\) at stimuli \(j\) (averaged over subjects in that group).

Key terms: fixed vs main vs simple effects (what the questions are asking)

  • Fixed effect: an effect of a factor whose levels are the specific conditions of interest (not a random sample of levels). In SG3 Problem 6, damage and stimuli are treated as fixed factors, so α, β, and αβ are fixed effects.
  • Main effect: the effect of a factor averaged over the other factor(s). Example: a stimuli main effect compares digits vs letters vs mixed after averaging across damage groups.
  • Simple effect: the effect of one factor at a specific level of another factor. Example: the damage effect within Mixed stimuli.
  • Interaction: whether the effect of one factor changes across levels of the other factor (a “difference of differences”).

Rule of thumb: if you see a contrast defined on the cell means, it’s often a simple-effect or interaction-style question (6c).

Standard fixed-effect identities (labeled)

  • Eq. 1 (Damage main-effect deviation): \(\alpha_i = \bar Y_{i\cdot\cdot} - \bar Y_{\cdot\cdot\cdot}\)
  • Eq. 2 (Stimuli main-effect deviation): \(\beta_j = \bar Y_{\cdot j \cdot} - \bar Y_{\cdot\cdot\cdot}\)
  • Eq. 3 (Interaction cell deviation): \((\alpha\beta)_{ij} = \bar Y_{ij\cdot} - \bar Y_{i\cdot\cdot} - \bar Y_{\cdot j \cdot} + \bar Y_{\cdot\cdot\cdot}\)
  • Eq. 4 (Mean square): \(MS = SS/df\) (turn SS into a per-df variance estimate)
  • Eq. 5 (F ratio): \(F = MS_{effect}/MS_{error}\) (signal-to-noise, using the design’s correct error term)

Split-plot nuance: Eq. 5 uses different error terms for between- vs within-subject effects (spelled out in 6b).

6a — Fixed effects (compute α, β, αβ)

Mini-lesson: what does the question mean by an “effect”?

In this problem, an “effect” is a fixed-effect deviation (a mean difference) defined by the standard ANOVA identities (see Eq. 1–3 above). Even though the design is split-plot, the definitions of the effects are the same:

  • Between main effect (damage): \(\alpha_i = \bar Y_{i\cdot\cdot} - \bar Y_{\cdot\cdot\cdot}\)
  • Within main effect (stimuli): \(\beta_k = \bar Y_{\cdot\cdot k} - \bar Y_{\cdot\cdot\cdot}\)
  • Cell interaction effect: \((\alpha\beta)_{ik} = \bar Y_{i\cdot k} - \bar Y_{i\cdot\cdot} - \bar Y_{\cdot\cdot k} + \bar Y_{\cdot\cdot\cdot}\)

Meaning of each requested quantity:

  • \(\alpha_{right}\): how far the Right marginal mean is above/below the grand mean.
  • \(\beta_{mixed}\): how far the Mixed marginal mean is above/below the grand mean.
  • \((\alpha\beta)_{right,mixed}\): the (Right,Mixed) cell’s deviation from what you’d expect from adding the two main effects.

Where split-plot matters: choosing the correct error term later (6b/6c).

Try it first:
  • Use the damage marginal mean for “Right.”
  • Use the stimuli marginal mean for “Mixed.”
  • Use the Right×Mixed cell mean for the interaction effect.

Given: grand mean = 7.48.

Short answer (6a):

  • αright = 7.61 − 7.48 = +0.13
  • βmixed = 7.11 − 7.48 = −0.37
  • (αβ)right,mixed = 7.17 − 7.61 − 7.11 + 7.48 = −0.07

6b — Fill in the ANOVA table (split-plot error terms)

What does “fill in the ANOVA table” mean?

An ANOVA table is just a structured summary of variability. For each Source, you compute (see Eq. 4–5):

  • SS (sum of squares): how much variability is attributed to that source.
  • df (degrees of freedom): how many independent pieces of information that SS is based on.
  • MS (mean square): \(MS = SS/df\).
  • F (test statistic): \(F = MS_{effect}/MS_{error}\) using the correct error term for the design.

In split-plot designs the “correct error term” is the whole point: between-subject and within-subject effects do not share the same denominator.

Prompt (SG3)

Fill in this ANOVA table. Given SS: Damage=57; Stimuli=9; Subject=17.4; Damage×Stimuli=2; Subject×Stimuli=30.6

Mini-lesson: the two error terms in a split-plot

Because damage is between-subject, its variability is evaluated against subjects-within-damage. Because stimuli is within-subject (repeated), it is evaluated against the subject-by-stimuli interaction.

  • Between-subject F tests: divide by \(MS_{S(A)}\) (subjects within damage).
  • Within-subject F tests (stimuli, A×B): divide by \(MS_{B\times S(A)}\).
Try it first: before calculating anything, answer these two questions.
  1. What is the df for damage?
  2. What is the df for the within-subject error (stimuli×subject within damage)?

6c — Contrasts (type, null, Tukey vs Scheffé, interpret)

Prompt (SG3)

For each contrast: (a) identify the type of contrast; (b) write the null hypothesis; (c) calculate the appropriate posthoc-adjusted critical value; and (d) interpret the contrast, including directionality. Use Tukey for pairwise; Scheffé for complex.

Mini-lesson: what does “contrast” mean?

A contrast is a weighted combination of means (a linear combination). If the weights sum to 0, it tests a specific comparative statement about means (a “pattern” among means).

Generic form: \(L = \sum_j c_j\mu_j\), with \(\sum_j c_j = 0\).

  • Pairwise contrast (two means): weights like \((1,0,-1)\) compare two levels directly → use Tukey.
  • Complex contrast (≥3 means, average-vs-mean, or interaction-style difference-of-differences) → use Scheffé.

In the SG3 output, the key columns are: estimate (the contrast value), SE, and t.ratio (estimate/SE). You decide significance by comparing \(|t|\) to an adjusted critical value.

Step 1 (identify what each contrast compares):
  • c(-2,1,1) on damage means = compare Left to the average of Right and Control.
  • c(1,0,-1) on stimuli means = compare Digits vs Mixed (Letters is not used).
  • c(-2,1,1,0,0,0,0,0,0) = the same Left vs avg(Right,Control) comparison within Digits.
  • c(0,0,0,0,0,0,-2,1,1) = the same comparison within Mixed.
  • c(-2,1,1,0,0,0,2,-1,-1) = a difference of those two simple-effects (an interaction-style contrast).
Show null hypotheses (write them cleanly)
  • Damage contrast (-2,1,1): \(H_0: -2\mu_L + \mu_R + \mu_C = 0\), equivalently \(\mu_L = (\mu_R+\mu_C)/2\).
  • Stimuli contrast (1,0,-1): \(H_0: \mu_{digits} - \mu_{mixed} = 0\).
  • Digits simple-effect contrast: \(H_0: \mu_{L,digits} = (\mu_{R,digits}+\mu_{C,digits})/2\).
  • Mixed simple-effect contrast: \(H_0: \mu_{L,mixed} = (\mu_{R,mixed}+\mu_{C,mixed})/2\).
  • Interaction contrast: \(H_0: [\mu_{L,digits} - (\mu_{R,digits}+\mu_{C,digits})/2] - [\mu_{L,mixed} - (\mu_{R,mixed}+\mu_{C,mixed})/2] = 0\).
Show posthoc critical values (Tukey vs Scheffé)

Using α = .05 and the df shown in SG3’s contrast output (df = 15):

  • Tukey (pairwise among 3 stimuli levels): use the studentized-range critical value \(q\). For k = 3, df = 15: \(q_{.95}\approx 3.67\), so \(t_{crit}\approx q/\sqrt{2} \approx 2.60\).
  • Scheffé (complex): \(t_{crit} = \sqrt{(m-1)F_{.95, m-1, df}}\).
    • For 3 damage means (m = 3): \(t_{crit}\approx 2.71\).
    • For a contrast spanning 6 cell means (m = 6): \(t_{crit}\approx 3.81\).

You compare the absolute t.ratio to the appropriate adjusted \(t_{crit}\).

Show interpretations (directionality + decision)
Damage contrast (complex → Scheffé, m = 3):

Estimate = 3.94, t = 6.335. Since \(|t|=6.335\) > \(t_{crit}\approx 2.71\), reject \(H_0\).

Direction: the contrast is (Right + Control) − 2·Left, so a positive estimate means Right/Control average is higher than Left. Conclusion: Left-damage subjects recall fewer characters than the average of Right-damage and Control.

Stimuli contrast (pairwise digits vs mixed → Tukey):

Estimate = 0.944, t = 3.034. Since \(|t|=3.034\) > \(t_{crit}\approx 2.60\), this difference is significant under Tukey.

Direction: digits − mixed is positive → recall is higher for digits than mixed (averaged over damage groups).

Digits simple-effect (complex among 3 damage groups → Scheffé, m = 3):

t = 3.424 > 2.71 → significant. Direction: (Right+Control) average exceeds Left within digits.

Mixed simple-effect (complex among 3 damage groups → Scheffé, m = 3):

t = 3.601 > 2.71 → significant. Direction: (Right+Control) average exceeds Left within mixed.

Interaction-style contrast (complex across cells → Scheffé, m = 6):

t = −0.505. Since \(|t|=0.505\) < \(t_{crit}\approx 3.81\), fail to reject \(H_0\).

Interpretation: the Left-vs-(Right/Control) gap does not differ meaningfully between digits and mixed.
