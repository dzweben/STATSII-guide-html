Objective 1 — Interpret all figures and tables in the Simmons, Nelson, and Simonsohn article.

This question asks you to interpret the figures and tables from Simmons, Nelson, and Simonsohn (2011). Below, each figure/table is shown as an image, followed immediately by a clear interpretation in words. Read each interpretation as: (1) what is being compared, (2) what the numbers show, and (3) why it matters for false positives.

Table 1 Interpretation

Table 1 is the paper’s core error‑rate message: the familiar “5% false positive” promise of p<.05 only holds for a single, pre‑specified analysis. Researcher “degrees of freedom” (try more than one DV, add/drop covariates, peek and stop early, decide which conditions to include, etc.) create a set of parallel hypothesis tests or “forking paths.” Even if each individual test uses the same .05 threshold, the probability that at least one path looks significant increases—this is the same logic as multiple comparisons / familywise error, just created by workflow rather than by an explicit list of tests. The numbers in the table quantify how quickly calibration breaks: e.g., analyzing multiple DVs (plus a combined score) raises the chance of a false positive to 9.5%, and stacking several common flexibilities pushes it to 60.7% (~61%). Interpretively, that means “a significant result” is no longer strong evidence against the null unless the analysis plan was constrained (pre‑registered) or all analytic choices are transparently reported so readers can assess how many chances the noise had to win.

Figure 1 Interpretation

Figure 1 is about sequential testing (a.k.a. “optional stopping” / “peeking”). Under the null hypothesis, a single, pre‑planned test at the end has a 5% chance of producing p<.05. But if you look at the data repeatedly as N grows and you adopt the rule “stop the moment p<.05,” you have turned one test into many looks at the same hypothesis. Conceptually, this is a multiple‑comparisons problem across time: each peek is another opportunity for random noise to cross the “significance” boundary. The figure shows that the overall false‑positive probability (familywise error across peeks) rises sharply with more frequent peeking, and it rises faster when you start with a smaller initial sample because early estimates are noisier (so the test statistic fluctuates more). In their simulation, starting at n=10 per condition and re‑testing after every added observation produces a false‑positive rate around 22%—meaning the stopping rule, not the science, is creating “discoveries.” The theoretical lesson: p-values are calibrated only for the sampling plan you actually used; changing the stopping rule changes the error rate.

Figure 2 Interpretation

Figure 2 gives the intuition for why Figure 1 happens. As you add participants and re‑run the same test, the estimated effect size and its standard error both change; the test statistic (and thus the p-value) is a moving target, not a monotone function of N. Under the null, the estimate is centered at 0 but it wanders randomly; as you accumulate data, the wander typically shrinks, yet the path can still cross the .05 threshold and later cross back. The key interpretive point is selection: if you only report the first time the path dips below .05, you are preferentially capturing a random downward fluctuation. That makes the reported result look “clean” (significant) even though, viewed as a full trajectory, it is just one noisy sample path that happened to hit the boundary. The theoretical lesson: “significant at some intermediate N” is not evidence that the effect will remain significant—unless the stopping rule was pre‑specified or the analysis properly adjusts for repeated looks (e.g., group‑sequential/alpha‑spending methods).

Table 2 Interpretation

Table 2 is not just a checklist—it’s an incentive/epistemology intervention. The authors’ claim is that false positives persist largely because the scientific record only shows the winning analytic path; readers cannot see how many alternative choices were tried (or could have been tried). The proposed reporting requirements (six for authors; four expectations for reviewers) aim to restore meaning to p-values by changing the information environment: disclose all conditions, all measures, the stopping rule, exclusions, and the role of covariates (including reporting the analysis without the covariate when applicable). Interpreted theoretically, transparency doesn’t magically prevent flexibility—it turns hidden multiplicity into visible uncertainty. Once the garden of forking paths is documented, a “significant” result can be evaluated as robust vs. fragile, and reviewers can demand robustness checks or replications when the justification for choices is weak. In other words: Table 2 is proposing a way to make exploratory flexibility compatible with honest inference.

Table 3 Interpretation

Table 3 (“Solutions in action”) is a concrete demonstration of the paper’s theory: how a result can look confirmatory when written up, yet be largely an artifact of selection among defensible choices. It rewrites their own Study 2 (“chronological rejuvenation,” using When I’m Sixty‑Four) in the “Table 2 compliant” style to show what gets hidden in a standard narrative: many additional measures that could have been analyzed, monitoring significance while collecting data (optional stopping), and dependence of the headline effect on an analytic tweak (including a covariate like father’s age). Interpretively, the point is not “covariates are bad” or “collecting many measures is bad”—it’s that when these choices are made after seeing data and only the successful combination is reported, the nominal p<.05 no longer represents a 5% error rate. The table forces you to see the result as one path through a larger space of possibilities, which reframes the appropriate conclusion: at best, an exploratory pattern that needs strong robustness/replication, not a clean discovery.

Objective 2 — List ways researcher discretion (“degrees of freedom”) increase false positives

Researcher degrees of freedom are the many reasonable decisions analysts can make after seeing the data (which outcome to emphasize, which covariates to include, when to stop, etc.). Each decision is a “fork in the road.” The crucial mechanism is: more forks = more chances for random noise to look “significant.”

Concretely, if the null hypothesis is actually true and you try k reasonable analyses but only report the one with p<.05, then you have effectively run a small family of tests. The probability of at least one false positive is approximately 1-(1-0.05)^k. So with k=10 forks, the chance of at least one “significant” result is about 1-0.95^{10}\approx 0.40 (40%)—even though each individual test used the same 5% threshold.

Common sources of flexibility (each adds forks) include: optional stopping (peek and stop when p crosses .05), analyzing multiple DVs and reporting only the significant one, trying different covariate sets, dropping/combining conditions, removing outliers after seeing results, trying alternative transformations/coding rules, and searching for subgroups/interactions.

Why this is sometimes described as “dishonesty”: the false positive rate increases when the workflow selectively reports the best‑looking path while hiding the number of paths tried. Even when each step sounds defensible in isolation, the selection is what breaks the “p<.05 → 5% false positive” calibration. Pre‑specifying choices (or transparently disclosing all choices tried) is what prevents that inflation.

Objective 3 — Calculate the fixed “effects” (α, β, αβ, e) for GLM / ANOVA designs

Example practice in this dashboard: P2e, P2f, P3a, P4a, P5b, P6a

An effect is a deviation from the grand mean. The basic idea is that each factor’s level mean can be expressed as the grand mean plus an effect term. Interaction effects capture the extra deviation beyond the main effects.

Two‑way between‑subjects model:
αj = Ȳj − Ȳ,   βk = Ȳk − Ȳ,   (αβ)jk = Ȳjk − Ȳj − Ȳk + Ȳ,   eijk = Yijk − Ȳjk

One‑factor within‑subjects model:
αj = Ȳj − Ȳ,   eij = Yij − Ȳj − Ȳi + Ȳ
Here the residual subtracts each person’s mean to remove stable individual differences.

Two‑factor within‑subjects (fixed effects only):
Use the same αj, βk, and (αβ)jk formulas above; subject terms are omitted per the prompt.

Split‑plot (one between‑subjects factor A, one within‑subjects factor B):
αj, βk, (αβ)jk as above;   eijk = Yijk − Ȳij − Ȳjk + Ȳj
This residual reflects within‑person variability, with subjects nested in the between‑subjects factor.

Objective 4 — Identify the correct error term for specific effects (RM ANOVA, split-plot)

Example practice in this dashboard: P5a, P6b

Each effect is tested against an error term that represents the variability not explained by that effect. In repeated‑measures and split‑plot designs, the correct error term depends on whether the effect is within‑subjects or between‑subjects.

Between‑subjects analysis: The error term is the within‑cell variability among individuals.

One‑factor within‑subjects analysis: The error term is the subject × factor interaction. It captures how much each person’s condition effect differs from the average condition effect.

Two‑factor within‑subjects analysis: The error for Factor A is A × Subject; the error for Factor B is B × Subject; the error for the A × B interaction is A × B × Subject.

Split‑plot analysis: The error for the between‑subjects factor is Subject (nested in the between‑subjects factor). The error for the within‑subjects factor and the interaction is the within‑subjects factor × Subject (nested).

Objective 5 — Explain error components conceptually (one-way and two-way within-subject)

Example practice in this dashboard: P5a, P6b (conceptual background)

In a one‑way within‑subjects design, each person provides multiple scores. Subtracting each person’s mean removes stable individual differences. What remains is the person’s fluctuation across conditions. That fluctuation is the subject × condition error term.

In a two‑way within‑subjects design, the same logic applies to each effect. The error for Factor A is how each person’s A‑effect deviates from the average A‑effect. The error for Factor B is how each person’s B‑effect deviates. The error for the interaction is how each person’s interaction pattern deviates from the average interaction.

Objective 6 — Between-subject vs within-subject ANOVA (design, ANOVA table, SS, F)

Example practice in this dashboard: P5a, P6b (design/ANOVA-table framing)

Design: Between‑subjects designs compare different people in each condition; within‑subjects designs measure the same people across conditions.

ANOVA table: Between‑subjects uses one error term (within‑cell variance). Within‑subjects partitions subject variability and uses subject × effect error terms.

Sums of squares: Within‑subjects removes subject sums of squares from the error, which usually lowers the error term and increases power. Between‑subjects does not.

F ratios: Between‑subjects uses mean square for the effect divided by mean square for within‑cell error. Within‑subjects uses mean square for the effect divided by mean square for subject × effect error (and requires sphericity or corrected degrees of freedom).

Objective 7 — Interpret R output for ANOVA designs (two-way, RM, split-plot)

Example practice in this dashboard: Standalone skill (helps you read R output; not directly required by the SG3 problems)

Start by identifying the effect rows (Factor A, Factor B, and the interaction) and their degrees of freedom. Then identify the correct error term used for each effect. In repeated‑measures output, the denominator is often a subject‑interaction term rather than within‑cell variance.

Next, check sphericity tests (such as Mauchly’s test). If sphericity is violated, use Greenhouse–Geisser or Huynh–Feldt corrected p‑values. Finally, interpret the direction of effects using marginal means or cell means and (if provided) plots.

Objective 8 — Main effects vs interaction effects; what “consistent main effect” means

Example practice in this dashboard: P2a, P2b, P2c, P2d

A main effect is the average difference between levels of one factor after collapsing across the other factor. An interaction exists when the effect of one factor changes depending on the level of the other factor (non‑parallel lines in a plot).

A consistent main effect means the direction and ordering of the effect are the same at every level of the other factor. If the ordering flips or the direction changes, the main effect is not consistent.

Objective 9 — Type I vs Type III (weighted vs unweighted marginal means) and what differences mean

Example practice in this dashboard: P1

Type I uses weighted marginal means based on the observed cell sizes, so it reflects the actual sample composition. Type III uses unweighted marginal means, which treat each cell as equally important and represent a balanced design.

If Type I and Type III differ, it means the other factor is imbalanced across groups. In that case, the Type I effect is partly driven by that imbalance, while the Type III effect is closer to the “pure” effect of the factor of interest.

Objective 10 — Compute/describe contrasts (main, simple, interaction) from ANOVA data

Example practice in this dashboard: P2g, P2h, P2i, P3b, P4b, P5c, P6c

A main‑effect contrast compares marginal means across levels of one factor (for example, Factor A level 1 vs level 2). A simple‑effect contrast compares levels of one factor within a fixed level of the other factor. An interaction contrast compares two simple effects (a difference of differences) to test whether the simple effects differ from each other.

Objective 11 — Apply post-hoc adjustments (Tukey vs Scheffé) across contrast types

Example practice in this dashboard: P2g, P2h, P2i, P3b, P4b, P5c, P6c

Use the Tukey adjustment for pairwise contrasts and the Scheffé adjustment for complex contrasts. Tukey uses the studentized range distribution; Scheffé uses the F distribution with degrees of freedom for the effect.

When doing simple effects, adjust the significance level by the number of simple‑effect tests (for example, divide α by the number of levels in the other factor). Interactions use the Scheffé adjustment with degrees of freedom equal to (a−1)(b−1).

Equations on the Test

Contrast test statistic (general form)

The contrast value is ψ̂ = Σ cj Ȳj. The test statistic compares ψ̂ to its standard error:

t = ψ̂ / √(MSE · Σ(cj2/nj))

Use the appropriate mean square error (from the correct error term) and the correct sample size for each level.

Effects (main and interaction)

αj = Ȳj − Ȳ   βk = Ȳk − Ȳ

(αβ)jk = Ȳjk − Ȳj − Ȳk + Ȳ

Adjusted critical values for contrasts

Tukey (pairwise): t* = qk, dferror, α′ / √2

  • k = number of means being compared (the “a” in SG3’s notation; e.g., k=4 in Problem 4).
  • α′ is whatever adjusted alpha SG3 uses for that family of tests (sometimes α, sometimes α/2, α/6, etc. depending on how many comparisons/tests are being controlled).

Scheffé (complex): t* = √(dfeffect · Fdfeffect, dferror, α′)

  • For a main-effect contrast across a factor with a levels, dfeffect = a−1.
  • For an interaction contrast in a two-way (a×b), dfeffect = (a−1)(b−1) (matches SG3’s “√(2·F…)” and similar patterns).

Problem 1 (Type I vs Type III; “Pure” Effects)

Full Prompt (SG3)

Here is data on the reading scores (converted to z-scores, so the unit of measurement is standard deviation) of first graders in 2013, by the education of the caretaking parent and income. The numbers in parentheses are cell sizes.

Calculate the difference between children from low-income families (<$75,000) and children from high-income families (>=$75,001) using the Type I and Type III approaches. Interpret the different effects. What conclusion do you make about the Type I effect of income—is it a “pure” income effect or not?

Parental Education<$75,000$75,001+
Less than BA-0.24 (n=6668)0.19 (n=1424)
BA+0.30 (n=1437)0.50 (n=2916)

Skills tested: Objective: Type I vs Type III

Answer (computed):

  • Type I (weighted) income difference: 0.54 SD (high − low)
  • Type III (unweighted) income difference: 0.32 SD (high − low)

Conclusion: The Type I effect is not a “pure” income effect here because the income groups have very different mixes of parental education (the cells are imbalanced). The Type III effect is closer to the “pure” income effect because it balances education across income by using unweighted marginal means.

Show solution walkthrough (how Type I vs Type III were computed)

Step 1: Type I (weighted) means. Compute weighted means using the cell sizes.

\(\bar Y_{\text{low}} = \dfrac{(-0.24)(6668) + (0.30)(1437)}{6668+1437} = -0.14\)

\(\bar Y_{\text{high}} = \dfrac{(0.19)(1424) + (0.50)(2916)}{1424+2916} = 0.40\)

Type I difference = 0.40 − (−0.14) = 0.54.

Step 2: Type III (unweighted) means. Average the two cell means for each income group (treat the design as if it were balanced across education).

\(\bar Y_{\text{low}} = \dfrac{-0.24 + 0.30}{2} = 0.03\)

\(\bar Y_{\text{high}} = \dfrac{0.19 + 0.50}{2} = 0.35\)

Type III difference = 0.35 − 0.03 = 0.32.

Why they differ (the “pure effect” idea): Type I uses the observed cell sizes, so the low‑income mean is pulled toward the “Less than BA” cell (huge n), while the high‑income mean is pulled toward the “BA+” cell (larger n). That means the income contrast is partly picking up education composition. Type III forces a 50/50 balance across education levels, so it isolates income more cleanly.

Problem 2a — Interpret the omnibus F-statistics

Prompt

Interpret the omnibus F-statistics in this table.

Answer (interpretation):

  • Interaction: treatment × orientation is highly significant, F(2,54)=16.72, p<.001 → the effect of officer race depends on political orientation.
  • Main effects: treatment is borderline, F(1,54)=4.01, p=.05; orientation is not significant, F(2,54)=2.00, p=.15.

Because the interaction is strong, you don’t treat either main effect as a single “one-size-fits-all” summary; you move to simple effects and the pattern of the cell means.

Show solution walkthrough (exactly what each F test is doing)

1) What an omnibus F test is: Each line is testing whether a set of population means are equal after accounting for the other terms in the model (Type III ANOVA).

2) Degrees of freedom:

  • Treatment has 2 levels → df_1 = 2-1 = 1
  • Orientation has 3 levels → df_1 = 3-1 = 2
  • Interaction has (2-1)(3-1)=2 numerator df
  • Error df is df_2 = N - ab = 60 - 6 = 54 (two-way between-subjects)

3) What the F ratio compares: In a between-subjects ANOVA, the denominator is the same error term for all effects: MSE. Formally:

\(F = \dfrac{MS_{effect}}{MSE}\)

So with MSE=1.73 you can recover (if you want) the mean-square for each effect, e.g.:

  • MS_{treatment} = 4.01\times 1.73 \approx 6.94
  • MS_{interaction} = 16.72\times 1.73 \approx 28.93

4) How to interpret “interaction significant”: It means the treatment difference is not constant across ideology. The next step is to look at the cell means (and/or compute simple effects/contrasts), not to summarize with one main effect.

Show solution walkthrough (how α, β, αβ are computed from the means table)

Model reminder (Objective 3): In a two-way between-subjects fixed-effects ANOVA, the cell mean is decomposed as:

\(\bar Y_{jk} = \bar Y + \alpha_j + \beta_k + (\alpha\beta)_{jk}\)

where:

  • \(\alpha_j = \bar Y_{j\cdot} - \bar Y\) (row mean minus grand mean)
  • \(\beta_k = \bar Y_{\cdot k} - \bar Y\) (column mean minus grand mean)
  • \((\alpha\beta)_{jk} = \bar Y_{jk} - \bar Y_{j\cdot} - \bar Y_{\cdot k} + \bar Y\)

Step 1: Grand mean. The table gives \(\bar Y=2.95\).

Step 2: α (treatment) from row means.

  • Black row mean = 2.61 → αblack = 2.61 − 2.95 = −0.34
  • White row mean = 3.29 → αwhite = 3.29 − 2.95 = +0.34

Check: α’s should sum to 0 (−0.34 + 0.34 = 0).

Step 3: β (orientation) from column means.

  • βlib = 2.55 − 2.95 = −0.40
  • βmod = 3.38 − 2.95 = +0.43
  • βcons = 2.94 − 2.95 = −0.01

Check: β’s sum to ~0 (rounding makes it slightly off).

Step 4: αβ from each cell. Example (black, liberal):

\((\alpha\beta)_{black,lib}=3.19-2.61-2.55+2.95=0.98\)

Do the same subtraction for each of the 6 cells.

Interpretation note: Large positive/negative αβ values indicate where the cell mean is much higher/lower than what you’d predict from adding the main effects alone — i.e., the interaction pattern.

Definition (Objective 3): In a fixed-effects two-way ANOVA, the residual for person i in cell (j,k) is:

\(e_{ijk} = Y_{ijk} - \bar Y_{jk}\)

It is “how far that person’s score is from their cell mean.”

Here: (j,k) = (black officer, conservative). The cell mean from the table is 1.25. The person’s observed rating is 7. So the residual is 7 − 1.25 = 5.75.

Interpretation: This is a very large positive residual: this person rated “double jeopardy” much higher than the average conservative in the black-officer condition. In the ANOVA, the collection of these residuals contributes to the SS_{error} and therefore the MSE used in the F tests.
